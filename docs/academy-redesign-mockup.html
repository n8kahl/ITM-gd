Calls vs Puts: When to Use Each
Concept

A call option gives you the right to buy 100 shares of a stock at a specific price (the strike price) before a specific date (the expiration). You buy calls when you believe the underlying will move higher.

A put option gives you the right to sell 100 shares at the strike price before expiration. You buy puts when you believe the underlying will move lower.

The Key Decision Framework

Before choosing calls or puts, ask yourself three questions:

1. What is my directional thesis? If bullish, you're looking at calls. If bearish, puts. If neutral, neither ‚Äî consider spreads or stay flat.

2. What is my time horizon? Options decay over time (theta). The further out your expiration, the more time you buy, but the more you pay. Match your expiration to your thesis timeline.

3. What is my risk budget? The maximum you can lose on a long call or put is the premium you paid. Size accordingly ‚Äî never risk more than 1-2% of your account on a single options trade.

Rule
Never buy an option without a clear directional thesis and a defined exit. "It might go up" is not a thesis. "SPX is holding above the 50-day moving average with rising volume, targeting 4,200 by Friday expiration" ‚Äî that's a thesis.
